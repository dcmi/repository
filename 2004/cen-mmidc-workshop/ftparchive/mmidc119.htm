Foreword

 

The production of this CEN Workshop Agreement (CWA) was formally accepted as part of the CEN/ISSS Workshop on Metadata for Multimedia Information - Dublin Core (WS/MMI-DC) in the Workshop's plenary meeting on 2004-01-18.

This CWA was agreed upon by the contributing partners in the CEN/ISSS Workshop on MMI-DC, representing a wide mix of interests, including administrations, libraries, on-line education and geographic information systems. The list of company individuals who have supported the document's contents may be obtained from the CEN/ISSS Secretariat.

 

1                                Scope

The present document gives guidance on the deployment of the EU e-Government Metadata Framework. The Framework itself is reported in ISSS/WS-MMI-DC/118.

This document is the result of co-operation between the members of the CEN/ISSS MMI-DC workshop in the period between January 2004 and December 2004 and capitalises on work reported in CWA 14859 [1] and CWA 14860 [2].

The present document is applicable to three main categories of people:

1)         strategic decision makers

2)         policy makers

3)         metadata providers

Vendors of systems and software will not be addressed directly but it is conceivable that they will also benefit from the material.

The aim of this document is to provide usable guidelines for Members States that wish to adopt a European dimension to their metadata standards. For Member States with a national metadata standard is place, this document does not suggest replacing it but rather complementing it with a European dimension. For Member States without a national metadata standard, this document suggests the use of the Application Profile proposed in CWA 14860.

The document further aims to provide an initial view of deploying the EU eGovernment Metadata Framework reported in ISSS/WS-MMI-DC/118. We acknowledge that establishing the necessary organisational and technical infrastructure is a non-trivial task. It is therefore advised that further work is performed in this direction. In this respect, it is suggested that a pilot would provide valuable feedback as to the efficient and effectively of the proposed approach.

Finally, the contents and relationships between the four CWAs on e-Government metadata are as follows:

  • CWA 14860 presents a proposed e-Government metadata application profile. This is based on DC and MIReG and includes elements and element refinements found in national standards of UK, Ireland, Denmark, Finland, Iceland, Canada, Australia and New Zealand.
  • CWA 14859 provides guidelines for Member States that do not currently have a metadata standard in place.
  • CWA-A (draft: ISSS/WS-MMI-DC/118) number of metadata-related standards and proposes a general framework for e-Government metadata in Europe. The aim of this framework is to enable interoperability between possibly different metadata standards in various Member States. This is achieved by adopting the application profile reported in CWA 14860 as a common bridge that allows building one multilateral solution instead of many bi-lateral ones.
  • CWA-B (this draft) presents simple deployment guidelines of the framework reported in CWA-A (draft: ISSS/WS-MMI-DC/118). It provides guidelines for the central authority envisaged and for Member States with and without national standards in place. It also lists relevant standards that could be used by Member States in their relevant tendering processes.

3                                The eGovernment Metadata Framework

3.1                       Introduction

This section outlines the proposed e-Government Metadata Framework. The framework is presented in detail in CWA An eGovernment Metadata Framework.

3.2                       Rationale and Scope

European public bodies are by far the largest producers of information [5]. The European Commission with a recent directive aimed at opening up public content for commercial exploitation [6]. Furthermore, the eEurope 2005 initiative suggests that e-Government should be a priority for Member States.

The use of metadata is an essential pre-requisite for facilitating citizens to discover and retrieve governmental resources and for exploitation of these resources by businesses. According to the European Interoperability Framework for pan-European eGovernment Services by the European Commission [7], metadata are essential in achieving semantic interoperability of e-services at a European level. In the same report, it is recommended that initiatives at pan-European level to develop common semantics on the basis of XML should be performed in a co-ordinated way, and should consider co-operation with the existing standardization bodies.

The Dublin Core Metadata Initiative is currently updating a simple, yet concise, element set that can be used as a basis from different communities according to their needs. In the domain of e-Government, different countries have specified and implemented their e-Government metadata standards based on Dublin Core. At the European level, the IDA MIReG project has proposed a European metadata standard and implemented a software tool that can be used by administrations for experimentation [8].

The work of the European Committee of Standardization aims to fill in the gap that exists at the European level for a simple e-Government Metadata Framework and supporting deployment guidelines. The aim of this framework in not to replace national initiatives. It is rather to complement them with a European dimension thus ensuring interoperability at a European scale and beyond.

3.3                       Overview of the Framework

The proposed e-Government Metadata Framework suggests a hierarchical approach with two layers, as shown in the following figure. At the upper layer is a European Metadata Authority that maintains a European Application Profile. At the lower layer are Metadata Authorities of all Member States and possibly other governments that wish to participate.

 

The communication between the European Authority and the Member States Authorities is performed using the Metadata Interoperability Interface (MII) in direct analogy with the Business Interoperability Interface proposed in the European Interoperability Framework for pan-European eGovernment Services.

The same structure also indicates the relationship between different metadata standards. There is one European Metadata Standard that is maintained by the European Metadata Authority, and national metadata standards that are maintained by metadata authorities of the Member States.

The main requirement is that the national standards are always in synchronisation with the European standard. Thus, the national MII should include technological bridges that enable interoperability with the European standard and through it with other Member States.

In some Member States the process of specifying a metadata standard is still in its early steps. It is advised that these Member States directly adopt the European standard thus preserving resources and ensuring interoperability at a European level.

It should be noted that the use of the European Metadata Standard is not restricted to Member States. Instead its potential use is also extended to EU Institutions including the European Commission. In that respect, all guidelines that are presented in this document for Member States are also valid for EU Institutions. However, the term Member States will be maintained to better reflect the main audience of this document.

3.4                       Overview of Recommendations

The framework is complemented with a set of recommendations divided into organisational, technical and semantic. These include:

Overall Recommendations:

  • Recommendation O1. (Overall organisational)

A European Metadata Authority should be established. Member States should establish National Metadata Authorities, if they have not already. Member States to inform the European Authority of one National Authority responsible for Metadata.

  • Recommendation O2. (Overall semantic)

The European Metadata Authority, in collaboration with the Member States Metadata Authorities, should adopt a set of rules for establishing, documenting and maintaining a European e-Government Metadata Standard.

  • Recommendation O3. (Overall organisational)

The European Metadata Authority and the Member States Metadata Authorities should agree on the necessary Metadata Interoperability Interfaces through which their relevant business processes will be able to interoperate at a pan-European level.

  • Recommendation O4. (Overall technical)

The European Metadata Authority and the Member States Metadata Authorities should agree on the technical details and functionality of the supporting software; this includes specifying the provided services.

 

Recommendations for the European Authority:

  • Recommendation E1. (European Authority semantic)

The Authority should adopt a set of rules for establishing, documenting and maintaining the European Metadata Standard.

  • Recommendation E2. (European Authority organisational)

The Authority should establish the necessary Metadata Interoperability Interfaces to support the business processes agreed with the Member States Metadata Authorities.

  • Recommendation E3. (European Authority technical)
  • The Authority should maintain proper implementation of all metadata terms in the European Metadata Standard in XML and/or RDF.
  • Recommendation E4. (European Authority technical)

The Authority should implement and maintain a European Software Gateway that enables two-way communication between software components at MMI of the different Member States. This communication should support all agreed functionalities and should be fully multilingual.

 

Recommendations for the Member States Authorities:

  • Recommendation MS1. (MS Authority semantic)

The Member States Authority is responsible for ensuring that the national standard is compliant to the European Standard at all times.

  • Recommendation MS2. (MS organisational)

The Member States Authority should establish the necessary Metadata Interoperability Interfaces to support the business processes agreed with the European Metadata Authority.

  • Recommendation MS3. (MS technical)

The Member States Authority should be responsible for the development and maintenance of a National Software Gateway that enables two-way communication between national and European Authority software components. This communication should support all agreed functionalities and should be fully multilingual.

 

4                                General Deployment Guidelines

4.1                       Introduction

An e-Government Metadata Framework is, in many cases, part of an e-Government Interoperability Framework. This clause starts by presenting some recommendations on developing a national e-Government Interoperability Framework and some principles of existing frameworks.

The proposed e-Government Metadata Framework capitalises on a number of standards. This clause also includes some general principles on the frameworks deployment emphasising the importance of standards. The work reported in this clause has been influenced by relevant standardisation activities, activities within IDA and activities within the Unite Kingdom.

4.2                       Metadata Framework and Interoperability Framework

An e-Government Metadata Framework can be considered as part of an e-Government Interoperability Framework. As such, it inherits more general principles than are normally mentioned in the latter.

The European Commission suggests that:

When implementing a national interoperability framework the emphasis is obviously on interoperability. Standardization in technology and harmonization in legislation are just two ways to achieve this. Recommendations are:

    • Use open standards.
    • Incorporate existing standards in a larger context.
    • Stimulate re-use of proven standards.
    • Redesign administrative processes, make the best use of available technology. This is also an opportunity to make services more user-centred.
    • Keep administrative procedures independent from technology.
    • Co-ordinate and manage the e-Government initiative. Centrally agreed XML schemas may be provided free of charge throughout the public sector. This form of re-use reduces cost while the need to develop separate mechanisms for interchanging data is greatly reduced.
    • Keep track of developments in the wider community. For instance, changes in privacy legislation may impose requirements on the provision of some e-services.
    • Reduce the amount of data to be collected by using well-defined data dictionaries and data structures.
    • Ensure information security: prevent unauthorised access to systems and, in the case of highly confidential information, secure each record (or even each component) individually.
    • Enable wide access such as user-friendly interfaces, access for the disabled, foreign language support.

In the United Kingdom e-Government Interoperability Framework ver. 6.0, the following key policy decisions that have shaped the e-GIF are mentioned:

    • alignment with the Internet: the universal adoption of common specifications used on the Internet and World Wide Web for all public sector information systems.
    • adoption of XML as the primary standard for data integration and data management for all public sector systems.
    • adoption of the browser as the key interface: all public sector information systems are to be accessible through browser-based technology; other interfaces are permitted but only in addition to browser-based ones.
    • addition of metadata to government information resources.
    • development and adoption of the e-Government Metadata Standard, based on the international Dublin Core model (ISO 15836).
    • development and maintenance of the Government Category List.
    • adherence to the e-GIF is mandated throughout the public sector.
    • interfaces between government information systems and intermediaries providing e-Government services shall conform to the standards in the e-GIF. Interfaces between intermediaries and the public are outside the scope of the e-GIF.

The selection of e-GIF specifications has been driven by:

o        interoperability only specifications that are relevant to systems interconnectivity, data integration, e-services access and content management metadata are specified.

o        market support the specifications selected are widely supported by the market and are likely to reduce the cost and risk of government information systems.

o        scalability specifications selected have the capacity to be scaled to satisfy changed demands made on the system, such as changes in data volumes, number of transactions or number of users.

o        openness the specifications are documented and available to the public.

o        international standards preference will be given to standards with the broadest remit, so appropriate international standards will take preference over EU standards, and EU standards will take preference over United Kingdom standards.

4.3                       Considerations when Deploying the Metadata Framework

When deploying the European e-Government Metadata Framework, as with any national Metadata Framework, the following should be considered:

  • Implementation requirements
  • The tendering process
  • Communication with stakeholders

4.3.1                 Implementation requirements

The following list presents the implementation requirements of the UK Metadata Standard, as listed in the UK e-GIF ver. 6.0.

  • It will be independent; it will not be software, application or project-based.
  • It will be simple to use by those with widely varying experience of preparing resource descriptions.
  • It will be compliant with other UK Government standards and policies.
  • It will be compliant with international standards. Preference will be given to standards with the broadest remit, so appropriate international standards will take preference over EU standards, and EU standards will take preference over UK standards.
  • It will be stable. Changes to a standard that will become embedded in all information systems will require considerable effort, time and resources to implement.
  • It will be extensible. Additional element refinements can be added where it can be shown that these are essential. A balance will need to be struck between the need for extensibility and the need for stability.
  • It will be inclusive, taking into account the many existing content management metadata schemes, with the aim of minimising the need to rework existing products.
  • Above all, it will meet the information retrieval and management needs of government.

4.3.2                 The tendering process

The tendering process that is employed for developing a Metadata Framework is the responsibility of the relevant public authority according to the respective legislation and regulations.

Nevertheless, it is recommended that this tender include references to a number of standards to ensure interoperability and adherence to the recommendations of the European Commission. These include:

  • The Metadata Standard should be based on ISO 15836 (Dublin Core). It may have additional elements and refinements to meet the specialist needs of the public sector. It should be extensive and thus could be developed further as needs arise and encoding schemes become available.
  • Metadata definition should be based on the XML Schema mechanism, as specified by W3C.

4.3.3                 Communication with stakeholders

The opinion of stakeholders is particularly important for an e-Government Metadata Standard to achieve its goals. Stakeholders may include:

  • Suppliers such as software vendors and consultants who develop systems that should be implemented or comply to the framework.
  • Employees of public authorities working on metadata.
  • Citizens and businesses that will use the framework.

It is suggested that governmental agencies list the standards employed in metadata so as to facilitate suppliers. It is also suggested that the standards with regard to interfaces, data integration and supporting channels are also listed to facilitate intermediates that wish to use public sector content in order to provide value-added services and to better inform businesses and citizens.

 

 

5                                Specific Deployment Guidelines for the Framework Recommendations

5.1                       Introduction

The proposed e-Government Metadata Framework includes a number of recommendations. This clause includes some guidance for realising these recommendations.

The proposed e-Government Metadata Framework suggests that Member States Metadata Standards should be in synchronisation with the European Metadata Standard. In this clause, deployment guidelines are presented separately for Member States with a Metadata Standard in place and for those without a Metadata Standard already established.

5.2                       Overall Deployment Guidelines

The proposed e-Government Metadata Framework is a two-layer structure. At the upper layer rests a so-called European Metadata Authority while at the lower layer rest the Member States National Metadata Authorities and the Metadata Authorities within EU Institutions.

The establishment of this structure is a multi-faceted task with a number of dimensions including legal, political, budgetary and technological. Presenting an exhaustive list of all tasks that need to be performed is outside the scope of this document and the role of CEN MMI-DC Workshop. Thus, in this document the focus is the simple organisational, semantic and technical aspects.

The realisation of the whole structure is a task that needs to be driven and/or supported by the appropriate EU organisations and initiatives, such as DG Enterprise (IDAbc initiative) and DG Information Society (INFOSO). It is therefore recommended that metadata activities are included in the future work programmes of IDAbc and future call for proposals from INFOSO/e-Government.

As an example, a possible roadmap for implementing the supporting structure that is specified in the European eGovernment Metadata Framework is presented.

First of all, it is suggested that an approach with a minimum of two steps should be followed. The first step could contain a pilot system that includes only three to four Member States. The second step could include a full deployment of the framework in all Member States and all EU Institutes.

The first step could be performed as a tender by a suitable EU organisation or initiative. Prior to launching this tender, relevant stakeholders in the Member States and EU should be informed. Then a small number of Member States and EU Institutions that have shown interest in participating should be included in the pilot. It should be noted that ideally the Member States that will participate should represent a mixture of countries with and without metadata standards in place. This will ensure that the pilot represents a representative sample of the full-scale system. It is suggested that the pilot system includes, at a minimum, the European Metadata Authority, one EU Institution, two Member States with metadata standards in place and one Member State without a metadata standard in place.

The relevant tender of the pilot system could then include the following tasks:

  • Performing a number of studies such as review of e-Government metadata standards, cost-benefit analysis from the use of eGovernment metadata.
  • Creation of awareness in Member States and EU Institutes of the use and benefits of e-Government metadata.
  • Establishing the relevant committees and overall organisational structure as well as the processes for performing the work.
  • Establishing a set of rules for maintaining the European e-Government Metadata Standard. These rules should be agreed upon by the appropriate committees.
  • Specifying an appropriate e-Government Metadata Standard. Equally important is to document the standard properly and implement it in XML and RDF for example.
  • Specifying the functionality of the pilot system based on the requirements of stakeholders.
  • Implementing the pilot system. From a technical point of view, the European Metadata Authority could be established at the premises of the contractor. The implementation itself should respect all interoperability requirements as stated in the European Interoperability Framework for e-Government services. For example, it should be based on open standards, be portable to different platforms, be multilingual.
  • Deriving a trials plan and performing the trials of the pilot system according to the plan. During the trials, all necessary support should be provided by the contractor.
  • Disseminating the results to stakeholders using different channels.

The second step could be the full-scale development and deployment of the European e-Government Metadata Framework.

The relevant tender could include tasks such as the following:

  • Evaluation of the results of the pilot system.
  • Creation of awareness in Member States and EU Institutes of the use and benefits of e-Government metadata.
  • Update of the organisational structures to include representatives of all Member States and EU Institutes.
  • Full scale development and deployment including trials evaluation and viability analysis.

5.3                       Deployment Guidelines for the European Metadata Authority

Presenting a detailed list with requirements, legal, regulatory and organisational, for establishing a European Metadata Authority is outside the scope of this document and the role of CEN MMI-DC Workshop. In this clause, an example of some deployment guidelines for realising the first recommendation presented in the previous clause is given.

  • Recommendation E1. (European Authority semantic)

The Authority should adopt a set of rules for establishing, documenting and maintaining the European Metadata Standard.

  • Guidelines

The rules presented in CWA 14860 provide a starting point. These include:

1.       General Rules

1.1.    The application profile will be based on Dublin Core.

1.2.    Additions to Dublin Core will be only considered if they:

         have clear purpose

         provide clear value and

         do not significantly overlap with existing terms

1.3.    The application profile should allow harmonisation by allowing Member States to use their own labels for metadata terms, i.e. elements and refinements, but always using the centrally agreed name.

1.4.    All metadata terms in the application profile are considered as optional thus allowing Member States to determine the obligation of each metadata term according to their needs.

2.       First version and updates

2.1.    The first version of the e-Government Application Profile will be based on the metadata standards in Member States that are based on Dublin Core, i.e. United Kingdom, Denmark, Finland, Iceland and Ireland, while other relevant standards and initiatives will be also taken into account.

2.2.    All official changes of Dublin Core should be adopted immediately.

2.3.    For considering a new term, suitable documentation should be provided to indicate its purpose and value. For example, the documentation could be provided in the form proposed by the methodology reported in CWA 14859 Guidance on the Use of Metadata in e-Government.

 

The CEN e-Government metadata application profile ver. 1.0, presented in CWA 14860, could serve as a first version of the European metadata standard. Unlike other relevant work, this standard incorporates terms from most of the Member States standards thus facilitating the development of software bridges between that standard and Member States standards.

The Metadata terms could be documented using the attributes proposed in CWA Dublin Core Application Profile Guidelines. These are grouped into four categories as follows:

-          Identifying attributes: Term URI, Named, Label, Defined By.

-          Definitional Attributes: Definition, Comments, Type of term.

-          Relational Attributes: Refines, Refined By, Encoding Scheme For, Uses Encoding Scheme, Similar To.

-          Constraints: Obligation, Condition, Datatype, Occurrence.

5.4                       Deployment Guidelines for Member States with Metadata Standards in Place

The proposed e-Government Metadata Framework focuses on supplementing, rather then replacing, national metadata standards by adding the pan-European dimension. This approach is inline with the one followed by the European Interoperability Framework for pan-European e-Government Services.

Recommendations for the Member States Authorities:

  • Recommendation MS1. (MS Authority semantic)

The Member States Authority is responsible for ensuring that the national standard is compliant to the European Standard at all times.

  • Guidelines

First of all, it is noted that since the Member States in this category already have an e-Government metadata standard in place, thus they also have an Authority that is responsible for its maintenance. This Authority may be a committee or a Working Group within a bigger unit such as one working on Interoperability.

In any case, it is suggested that this Authority, in addition to its common duties at the national level, is also responsible for the pan-European dimension of metadata. Therefore, this Authority should also be responsible for ensuring that the country is represented at the European committees and that the national metadata standard is always in synchronisation with the European standard.

To achieve this goal, it is suggested that suitable actions are performed in this Authority. For example, new processes should be added to the existing ones to ensure that the national metadata standard is always in synchronisation with the European standard.

 

  • Recommendation MS2. (MS organisational)

The Member States Authority should establish the necessary Metadata Interoperability Interfaces to support the business processes agreed with the European Metadata Authority.

  • Guidelines

The Member States Authority should introduce a new organisational structure and/or relevant business processes that enable the communication with other Member States Authorities through the European Metadata Authority and according to the general rules that will be established.

 

  • Recommendation MS3. (MS technical)

The Member States Authority should be responsible for the development and maintenance of a National Software Gateway that enables two-way communication between national and European Authority software components. This communication should support all agreed functionalities and should be fully multilingual.

  • Guidelines

This technical recommendation could be fulfilled by in-house development of the Member States Authority or by a suitable tender. The approach to be followed depends on the role and technical capacity of the relevant Authority and it is up to each Member State to decide on the suitable approach.

5.5                       Deployment Guidelines for Member States without Metadata Standards in Place

 

A significant number of Member States have not yet adopted an e-Government metadata standard. These Member States should firstly evaluate the costs and benefits of the use of metadata in e-Government. A good starting point for this task is the section on Best Practices in CWA 14859.

 

Once the Member States favourably consider the establishment of a metadata standard, they should also consider its pan-European dimension. For example, Member States are advised to directly adopt the European e-Government Metadata Standard. The benefits from adopting the European standard include the following:

 

         the national standard is always highly relevant to the needs of all European stakeholders, since the European standard is expected to be regularly updated

         the national standard is supported by relevant software that is available at no cost

         interoperability at a European scale is guaranteed

         the costs are minimised

 

 

 

 

6                                Summary

This CWA presents a number of indicative deployment guidelines for implementing the European e-Government Metadata Framework that is reported in the relevant CWA. The document starts with an overview of the Framework, which suggests a two-layer structure with a single European Metadata Authority at the upper level and all other participating Authorities at the lower level. The metadata flow between a Public Authority of one Member State and a second Public Authority of another Member State is realised though the respective national Metadata Authorities and through the European Metadata Authority. This document continues with presenting deployment guidelines for realising the overall structure, for the European Metadata Authority and for the Member States Metadata Authorities. In all cases, the list is non-exhaustive and aims to facilitate the actions that will be finally taken by the responsible European and Member States organisations.
